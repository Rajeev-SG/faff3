# Market Findings: Productizing AI Delivery For Traditional Businesses

Date: 2026-03-22

## Executive summary

The strongest commercial wedge is not "AI agents" or "AI implementation" in abstract. It is helping traditional businesses move from scattered AI experiments to one clear, working, measurable result inside an existing workflow.

That proposition is supported by both the local repo evidence and current external research:

- McKinsey says the highest-value `Shaper` use cases are the ones where companies do more than buy a ready-made copilot. They integrate bespoke prompts, data, and system connections into a workflow that matters to the business.
- RSM's 2025 middle-market survey shows that adoption is high, but implementation friction is higher than expected, and 70% say they need outside help to get the most from AI.
- The detailed RSM breakdown makes the need for outside help concrete: businesses struggle with in-house expertise, identifying the right use cases, choosing the right technology, data quality, workflow integration, privacy/security, and compliance.
- OECD's 2025 SME work reinforces that smaller firms need support with the foundations: data and compute inputs, skills, finance, and clear adoption paths suited to their maturity.

## What McKinsey means by `Shaper` use cases

McKinsey contrasts two kinds of gen AI adoption:

- off-the-shelf use cases, where a company mainly adopts a vendor product such as Copilot or Salesforce Einstein
- `Shaper` use cases, where the company creates advantage by integrating prompts, data, and application connections into a business-specific workflow

Practical translation:

- A `Shaper` use case is not "we bought ChatGPT Teams."
- A `Shaper` use case is "we reduced quote turnaround from two days to two hours by connecting our knowledge, forms, CRM, and approval steps into one AI-assisted flow."

For faff3, this matters because your local tooling is much better suited to `Shaper` work than to reselling generic AI subscriptions.

## What the 70% need outside help for

RSM's topline says 70% need outside help. The more detailed industry infographic and survey notes show what that means in practice.

### Implementation problems businesses report

Among business and professional services respondents using generative AI, the biggest implementation challenges were:

- `40%` concerns about data privacy and security
- `40%` concerns about data quality
- `34%` difficulty integrating generative AI into existing operations and workflows
- `29%` concerns about intellectual property
- `26%` insufficient internal skills and expertise
- `26%` regulatory or compliance concerns

### Why firms felt underprepared

Among those that were not fully prepared for AI adoption, the top reasons were:

- `47%` lack of in-house expertise
- `42%` difficulty identifying the right AI use cases
- `33%` lack of clear AI strategy
- `33%` data quality challenges
- `33%` difficulty selecting the right AI technology for business needs

### Strategic interpretation

The outside help businesses want is not just prompt writing. It clusters into five needs:

1. `Choosing the right first use case`
   They do not want a generic innovation program. They want help picking one use case that is feasible, safe, and commercially meaningful.
2. `Connecting AI to the way the business already works`
   Integration into real workflows is a bigger blocker than model access.
3. `Making data usable enough for AI to be trusted`
   Data quality, permissions, and privacy are central blockers.
4. `Giving owners and managers a clear path to ROI`
   Buyers want visible time savings, faster turnaround, fewer handoff errors, or more conversions.
5. `Giving internal teams enough structure to keep going`
   Even interested businesses often lack internal skills, operating rules, and governance.

## What this means for package design

The offer ladder should be explained in business-owner language:

- first, identify the best use case
- then make one workflow actually work
- then roll that working pattern into the wider business
- then keep it useful, safe, and updated

This is a better fit than technical labels such as `agent enablement`, which sound developer-specific and can make a traditional business assume the service is not for them.

## Recommended positioning

Best positioning:

> We help businesses turn AI pilots into one clear, working result in a real workflow, then make that result repeatable.

Avoid leading with:

- agents
- MCP
- prompt engineering
- model selection
- "transformation"

Lead with:

- save admin time
- reduce handoff delays
- speed up quotes, reporting, or client responses
- connect the tools you already use
- show a clear before-and-after result

## Recommended package naming direction

Use plain-English names:

- `AI Workflow Review`
- `First Working AI Use Case`
- `Make AI Part of the Business`
- `Ongoing AI Support`

These names are understandable to a business owner who does not think in platform or engineering terms.

## Why the local repo work supports this

The local repos show repeatable capability in:

- defining a system of record for execution
- wiring tools and integrations into real workflows
- proving outcomes with acceptance and design evidence
- documenting rollout, rollback, and support expectations
- turning experiments into shippable, tracked, maintainable systems

That is exactly the capability middle-market and traditional-business buyers are struggling to build internally.
